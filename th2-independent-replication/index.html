◆ A QC guardrail caught a silent gene-label scramble

The arm scores turned out to be in a different row order than the annotation table (only 0.9% overlap) — attaching them naively mislabeled every gene, so the "top suppressors" came out as biological nonsense. A targeted 2.5 MB read of the atlas's row index (instead of the 16.8 GB matrix) recovered the true order. After the fix, the top hits are a clean cluster of chromatin/transcription regulators and GATA3 lands at #6 — the calibration that says the whole ranking can be trusted.

⚠ Finding: Th2 master regulators do not phenocopy each other

Knocking down GATA3 vs STAT6 vs IL4R produces nearly orthogonal transcriptomes (GATA3–STAT6 correlation ≈ −0.1). They're all "Th2 regulators" but act through distinct routes — so you can't validate a novel regulator just by transcriptome-similarity to GATA3. That tempered our confidence in one signal (coherence) and is itself a clean methodological result.

• The reproducible streaming pipeline — reads a 16.8 GB matrix over HTTP byte-ranges, no download. • The QC catch — a silent arm-to-annotation gene-scramble, fixed with a 2.5 MB read. • The calibrated negative result itself — Th1/Th2 are genuinely reciprocal, so single-gene "selective" suppression is rare and this screen + metric can't establish it. • Before any target claim: a functional IL-4/5/13 readout and a control-guide FDR — not more transcriptomic scoring.

01

The biology, from zero

Four ideas. That's the whole ladder you need to climb before the project makes sense.

1 · A CD4⁺ T cell is a conductor. It doesn't kill anything itself. When its receptor senses a threat, it commits to a role — and each role is defined by one master transcription factor (a gene that switches on a whole program) plus the signalling proteins (cytokines) it then secretes. Click a role to see what it does. Th2 is ours.

Interactive

The four fates of a helper T cell

2 · Th2 is the allergy program — and a proven drug target. Th2 cells drive asthma, eczema and allergic inflammation through IL-4, IL-5 and IL-13. The blockbuster drug dupilumab works by blocking exactly this signalling. So “a ranked list of genes whose removal quiets Th2” reads directly as “candidate targets for allergic disease.” That's the sentence that wins the Gladstone “overcome disease” award.

3 · Perturb-seq = break one gene per cell, then read the whole cell. You use CRISPR to silence a different gene in each of millions of cells, then sequence every cell's full set of active genes (its transcriptome). Each cell carries a barcode saying which gene was silenced. Aggregate the cells that share a target and you learn: “what does the cell's program do when gene X is gone?”

How the data is made

One experiment, millions of single-gene knockdowns

01

Pool

Millions of identical CD4⁺ T cells in one dish.

02

Silence

A virus drops one CRISPRi guide per cell — silencing one gene + leaving a barcode.

03

Stimulate

Activate the cells; sample at Rest, 8 h, 48 h.

04

Read

Sequence each cell → a cell×gene count matrix + its guide label.

05

Aggregate

Pool cells per guide → the average effect of losing that gene.

4 · Two catches that make this real work, not a lookup. First, this dataset's cells are only stimulated, never chemically forced into Th2 — so “the Th2 program” is a signature score you compute from marker genes, not a cell type you can see. Second, CRISPRi silencing is partial and only works for roughly 40–50% of targeted genes — so “no effect” is ambiguous until you check the knockdown actually happened. Both catches are where careful engineering beats naive analysis.

03

The wedge: their axis can't tell two things apart

Their model scores Th2-vs-Th1 as one number. Play with the toggle to see what that number hides.

The paper ranks regulators along a single bidirectional Th2-vs-Th1 axis. But “suppress Th2” and “flip the cell to Th1” are different biological events with the same score. For an allergy drug you want the first (quiet the allergy) and not the second (don't trade allergy for inflammation). A one-dimensional axis structurally cannot separate them. This plot can.

◆ Confirmed on real data (Haltavey's run)

Scoring every perturbation on the two arms: the selective quadrant is real — 172 knockdowns push Th2 down while Th1 stays flat, and true Th1-skewers are rare (~4). The surprise: the two arms are positively correlated (r ≈ +0.49) — the dominant axis across the screen is global response magnitude (cells broadly dampened), not polarization.

That's what makes the Th1 arm a two-sided internal control: “Th1 not down” removes the global-dampening artifacts (the real dominant confound), “Th1 not up” removes the skewers. One 2-D decomposition was meant to isolate Th2-specific suppression — but see the Results section: under a clean matched Th1-vs-Th0 arm it doesn't survive. The 2-D framing is a good idea this particular data + metric can't support.

07

Honest risk ledger

What could sink this, and the mitigation for each. Naming them is half the defense.

highOverlap with the paper

Their bidirectional axis already ranks Th2/Th1 regulators. Mitigation: never present an atlas — present the decoupled selective subset their axis conflates, with a diff against their published CSV.

highGlobal dampening — the confirmed dominant confound

Real data shows the arms correlate r ≈ +0.49: the biggest signal is cells getting broadly dampened, which drags the Th2 score down artifactually. Mitigation (now built in): the Th1 arm as a two-sided control kills it — “Th1 not down” removes dampeners — plus core-essential + knockdown-QC gates.

medNovel hits have no literature to lean on

Most hits aren't classic Th2 genes (expected for a genome-scale screen) — but novelty removes external corroboration. Mitigation: novel hits held to a higher internal-evidence bar — co-perturbation coherence with GATA3/STAT6 + donor reproducibility carry the proof. See §11③.

medFailed knockdowns pollute the ranking

CRISPRi silences only ~40–50% of targets. Mitigation: hard signif_knockdown gate before any ranking (Phase 1).

medTh2 signal is weak at Rest

The program barely exists in unstimulated cells, so “context-dependence” can be an artifact of the program only turning on when stimulated. Mitigation: interpret Rest vs Stim carefully; anchor claims in the 8 h / 48 h conditions.

medMechanism layer hallucinates

The agent could invent a plausible-sounding path. Mitigation: every edge checked against a structured source, tagged known vs speculative, with a citation — the ledger is the defense.

highPPI path-tracing is easy to undercut

GATA3 is a hub — a short PPI path to it exists from almost any gene (study-bias artifact), and PPI edges are undirected + ~80% incomplete. Mitigation: co-perturbation structure is primary evidence; PPI is corroboration only, used via propagation not shortest-path. See §08.

◆ Two genuinely-open pieces — bet the project on these

1 · The cheapest discriminating experiment. Everyone else (BioDiscoveryAgent, PerTurboAgent, Robin) proposes batches to maximize discovery — the opposite goal. Nobody optimizes the one minimal-cost experiment that separates two competing mechanisms.

2 · The “observed-in-our-data” tag. Fusing the literature/DB evidence ledger with your own Perturb-seq observation as a first-class known / speculative / observed label. Existing ledgers (INDRA) carry belief scores but no such tag.

Everything else is built — INDRA (typed-claim ledger + signed causal path-finding), POPPER (falsification), PaperQA2 / ContraCrow (literature + contradiction), GeneAgent (claim-checking). Run first — the Phase 0–1 starter

The one check that de-risks everything: do selective Th2 suppressors actually exist in the data? Paste into Claude Science's kernel; adapt names after inspecting.

selective_th2.py · Claude Science / scanpy
# ============================================================
#  Phase 0-1 starter · selective Th2-suppressor discovery
#  Marson genome-scale CD4+ T-cell Perturb-seq  (CZI VCP)
#  Paste into Claude Science's kernel; adapt names after inspecting.
# ============================================================
import scanpy as sc, pandas as pd, numpy as np, yaml
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
BASE = "data/"          # your download path

# ---- STEP 0 · get the data (once, in a terminal) -----------
#  aws s3 ls --no-sign-request s3://genome-scale-tcell-perturb-seq/marson2025_data/
#  need: DE_stats.h5ad, guide_kd_efficiency.suppl_table.csv,
#        Th2_Th1_polarization_signature_DE_results_full.suppl_table.csv,
#        th1_th2_known_regulators.yaml, core_essentials_hart.tsv

# ---- STEP 1 · load DE stats (one row per perturbation x condition)
de = sc.read_h5ad(BASE + "GWCD4i.DE_stats.h5ad")
print(de); print(de.obs.columns.tolist())     # INSPECT — names below may differ
#  .X (or a layer) = per-gene DE z-score for that perturbation

# ---- STEP 2 · build the DECOUPLED Th2 and Th1 gene sets -----
sig = pd.read_csv(BASE + "Th2_Th1_polarization_signature_DE_results_full.suppl_table.csv")
sig = sig.dropna(subset=["zscore"])            # +zscore = Th2-associated, - = Th1-associated
present = set(de.var_names); TOPN = 50
th2_genes = [g for g in sig.sort_values("zscore", ascending=False)["gene"] if g in present][:TOPN]
th1_genes = [g for g in sig.sort_values("zscore", ascending=True )["gene"] if g in present][:TOPN]

# ---- STEP 3 · score every perturbation on BOTH programs -----
#  effect = mean DE z-score across the program's genes.
#  th2_effect < 0  =>  the knockdown SUPPRESSES Th2 (what we want)
th2 = de[:, th2_genes].to_df().mean(axis=1)
th1 = de[:, th1_genes].to_df().mean(axis=1)
res = de.obs.copy(); res["th2_effect"] = th2.values; res["th1_effect"] = th1.values

# ---- STEP 4 · quality gates (drop untrustworthy rows) -------
ess = set(pd.read_csv(BASE + "core_essentials_hart.tsv", sep="\t", header=None)[0])
res = res[res["ontarget_significant"] == True]     # the knockdown had a real effect
res = res[res["offtarget_flag"] == False]          # no off-target contamination
res = res[~res["target"].isin(ess)]                # not core-essential (viability)
#  also require signif_knockdown by merging guide_kd_efficiency on target+condition

# ---- STEP 5 · the SELECTIVITY metric (the wedge) -----------
MIN_SUPPRESSION, MAX_TH1 = 0.40, 0.25
res["selective"] = (res.th2_effect <= -MIN_SUPPRESSION) & (res.th1_effect.abs() <= MAX_TH1)
res["score"] = (-res.th2_effect) - res.th1_effect.abs()      # reward Th2 down, punish Th1 move

# ---- STEP 6 · known (calibration) vs novel (the finding) ---
known = set(yaml.safe_load(open(BASE + "th1_th2_known_regulators.yaml"))["th2"])
sel = res[res.selective].sort_values("score", ascending=False)
sel["bucket"] = np.where(sel["target"].isin(known), "known", "novel")
recall = sel[sel.bucket == "known"]["target"].nunique() / len(known)
print(f"selective hits: {len(sel)}  |  known regulators recovered: {recall:.0%}")
print(sel[["target","culture_condition","th2_effect","th1_effect","bucket"]].head(30))

# ---- STEP 7 · the real selectivity plane (the payoff) ------
plt.scatter(res.th1_effect, res.th2_effect, s=6, alpha=.3)
plt.axhline(-MIN_SUPPRESSION, ls="--"); plt.axvline(0)
plt.xlabel("Th1 effect"); plt.ylabel("Th2 effect  (down = suppressed)")
#  >>> is the bottom-centre zone populated (selective suppressors exist),
#      or is it all a diagonal (Th2-down coupled to Th1-up)?  Either is a result.
The payoff is STEP 7: if the bottom-centre zone is populated, selective suppressors exist — that's your finding. If everything falls on a diagonal (Th2-down always drags Th1 up), the finding flips to “Th2 suppression is intrinsically coupled to Th1 gain in CD4 T cells.” Both are real, defensible results — which is how you know the question is well-posed.

② The cheapest-discriminating-experiment optimizer

Given a hit with two competing mechanisms, score every feasible assay by how differently the two hypotheses predict its readout, per unit cost — return the winner. Nobody else does this; everyone else proposes batches to maximize discovery.

discriminate.py · pseudocode
# two competing mechanistic hypotheses for one hit
H1 = "acts through GATA3"       # predicts: GATA3 protein down; rescue restores IL4/5/13
H2 = "acts parallel to GATA3"   # predicts: GATA3 protein flat; rescue does nothing

best = None
for e in ASSAY_LIBRARY:                     # each assay e has a cost and a readout
    p1 = predict_readout(e, H1)             # distribution H1 expects
    p2 = predict_readout(e, H2)             # distribution H2 expects
    discrimination = divergence(p1, p2)     # how differently they predict e
    score = discrimination / e.cost         # cheap AND separating
    best = keep_max(best, e, score)

return best   # the single cheapest experiment that splits H1 from H2
